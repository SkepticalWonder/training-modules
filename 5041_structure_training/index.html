Module Goals

By the end, students should be able to:

  • Explain the difference between strength, stiffness, weight, and accuracy.
  • Trace load paths through a robot frame or mechanism.
  • Choose fabrication methods that match team tools and time.
  • Design for bumpers, wiring, maintenance, and impact before final build.
  • Use CAD as a communication, checking, and fabrication tool.
The Big Checklist

Every design review starts here

  • Strong enough?
  • Stiff enough?
  • Light enough?
  • Buildable accurately?
  • Repairable quickly?
  • Safe wire and mechanism space?
  • Survive defense?
  • Bumper room?
  • Current FRC rules?
  • Ready for inspection?

Training habit: ask these before CAD is “done,” before parts are cut, and again before competition.

1. Frame First

Start with the frame and load paths

“When this gets hit, where does the force go?”

Defense hit
Bumper
Frame rail
Crossmember

If the answer is “through a tiny bracket,” redesign it.

Frame design rules of thumb

Do

  • Keep the frame square.
  • Use crossmembers and gussets.
  • Mount bumpers early in the design.
  • Route forces through strong members.

Avoid

  • Unsupported brackets carrying major loads.
  • Hanging heavy mechanisms from weak plates.
  • Designing bumper mounts after the robot is assembled.
  • Forcing misaligned parts together.
Activity

Load Path Sketch

In pairs, pick one robot mechanism. Draw where the force travels during a hit, shot, climb, or intake collision.

  • Circle the weak link.
  • Add one gusset, crossmember, or mount change.
  • Share the before and after load path.
2. Stiffness

Accuracy needs stiffness

Strong parts do not always make an accurate robot. A part can survive and still flex enough to miss shots, bind an elevator, or throw off alignment.

Areas that must stay stiff

Shooters

Consistent angle and wheel spacing.

Elevators / Arms

Parallel rails, stable pivots, hard stops.

Vision / Sensors

No wobble, protected mounting.

Swerve / Drivetrain

Square frame and rigid module mounts.

Belts / Chains

Aligned shafts and tension control.

Climbers

Loads travel into the frame, not thin side plates.

Stiffness quick fixes

  • Shorten unsupported spans.
  • Add triangles: gussets, diagonal bracing, boxed structures.
  • Use tube instead of flat plate where possible.
  • Support shafts on both sides.
  • Use hard stops instead of relying on motor limits alone.

4. Maintenance

Design so pit repairs are boring

Great robots fail less, but they still get hit. Good structure makes broken parts easy to reach, remove, and replace.

  • Battery access
  • Main breaker access
  • Motor controller access
  • Removable panels
  • Clear wire paths
  • Replaceable intake parts

Standardize to move faster

  • Use repeated bolt and rivet sizes.
  • Use common gussets and brackets.
  • Label wires, tubes, and devices.
  • Keep spare brackets, rollers, shafts, and intake pieces ready.
  • Use the same hole patterns when possible.
Pit Drill

Three-minute repair check

Look at a mechanism and ask: “Could we replace this between matches?”

  • What tool is needed?
  • What must be removed first?
  • What spare part should exist?
5. Impacts

Anything outside the frame must have a plan

Strong, flexible, or easy to replace.

Protect the vulnerable parts

  • Keep wheels, belts, chains, sensors, and wires protected.
  • Keep fragile mechanisms inside the frame perimeter when possible.
  • Use hard stops to prevent arms and intakes from overtraveling.
  • Avoid long, thin, unsupported parts sticking out.
  • Mount bumpers securely and legally.
6. Fabrication Accuracy

Design around the tools we actually have

Good fabrication starts in CAD. Accurate holes, square tube cuts, and repeatable jigs save hours during assembly.

Buildable design choices

  • Repeated hole patterns
  • Simple tube cuts
  • Few weird angles
  • Room for drills, riveters, wrenches, and hands
  • Parts easy to replace
  • Jigs for repeated drilling
  • Clamp before drilling
  • Match-drill paired plates
  • CNC/router/laser/template bearing holes
  • Check squareness during assembly

7. Weight Distribution

Low, centered, secure

Usually good

  • Battery low
  • Major masses near center
  • Even load on swerve modules
  • Heavy parts securely mounted

Watch closely

  • Arm pivots
  • Elevator uprights
  • Game piece storage
  • Thick plates and heavy material choices

Why it matters

  • High weight can make a tall robot tip.
  • Weight too far forward or backward can lift wheels.
  • Uneven swerve loading can make driving inconsistent.
  • Loose heavy parts become safety hazards.
8. CAD, CAD, CAD

CAD should answer practical questions

Use CAD to check dimensions, field constraints, mechanism travel, bumper space, electronics, battery location, and access before fabrication starts.

CAD checklist for 5041

  • Full frame modeled
  • Bumpers included
  • Major electronics included
  • Battery included
  • Frame perimeter checked
  • Mechanism travel checked
  • Bolt/rivet access checked
  • Assemblies used to test movement
  • Standard tube/gusset libraries used
  • Parts named clearly
CAD Review

Red Team / Blue Team

One group presents a mechanism. The other group tries to find failure points before anything is cut.

  • Where does it flex?
  • How is it repaired?
  • Where do wires go?
  • Does it leave bumper and inspection space?
Knowledge Check

Quick questions

  1. What is the difference between strong and stiff?
  2. Why should bumpers be designed early?
  3. Why should bearing holes be made with the most accurate process available?
  4. Name one reason a robot may drive poorly because of weight distribution.
  5. What part of your current design would be hardest to replace between matches?

Fabrication

Material, hole method, tools, jigs, assembly sequence.

Service
